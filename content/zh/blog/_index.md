I want to start with a confession: most of the technology news that matters to Taiwan is written for people who don't live here.
The big stories of the last few years — the AI race, the scramble for chips, the quiet expansion of state-backed cyber operations, the new rules Europe and the US are writing for data and platforms — all of these land on Taiwan harder than almost anywhere else on earth. We make the semiconductors the whole industry depends on. We sit on one of the most contested digital frontiers in the world. And yet when a Taiwanese reader wants to understand what just happened and why it matters to them, the best analysis is usually in English, behind a paywall, and framed for a Washington or London audience.
That gap is why I'm starting this blog.
What this blog is
[Blog Name] is a Mandarin-language publication covering global technology and cybersecurity for readers in Taiwan. The goal is simple to say and hard to do well: take the most important developments in tech and security — the ones being debated in San Francisco, Brussels, Tel Aviv, and London — and explain them clearly, accurately, and in a way that connects them to life on this island.
Not press-release rewrites. Not breathless gadget reviews. The aim is reporting and analysis that respects the reader's intelligence: what happened, who the players are, what's actually at stake, and what a thoughtful person in Taipei or Kaohsiung should take away from it.
I'm covering three things in particular.
First, the technology shaping the next decade — artificial intelligence, semiconductors, cloud infrastructure, and the companies and governments steering them. Taiwan is not a spectator to this story; it's a central character. I want our coverage to reflect that.
Second, cybersecurity and digital resilience. Taiwan absorbs an extraordinary volume of cyber intrusion attempts, disinformation, and infrastructure probing. Most citizens never see a clear, non-alarmist explanation of how these threats work or how to think about them. That's a public-interest gap worth closing.
Third, the rules of the digital world — how the EU's approach to AI and data, US export controls, and emerging global standards quietly reshape what Taiwanese companies and consumers can build, buy, and trust.
Why me, and why now
I'll be honest about where I am: this is a new project. I'm at the very beginning of building [Blog Name], writing the early articles, finding my voice, and learning what readers respond to. I don't have a masthead full of staff or years of archives behind me yet.
What I do have is the thing that started every good publication — a conviction that a specific audience is being underserved, and the willingness to do the unglamorous work of showing up and explaining things until I'm trusted to do it.
I'm choosing to write in Mandarin deliberately. There are excellent English-language tech outlets, and I read them daily. But language is not a neutral pipe; it shapes who gets to participate in a conversation. When the analysis only exists in English, an enormous number of curious, capable Taiwanese readers — students, small-business owners, public servants, retirees who grew up in a different technological era — are left to rely on translation and rumor. I'd rather meet them in the language they think in.
Why London Tech Week
This is where the press accreditation comes in, so let me be direct about my intentions.
London Tech Week, running 8–12 June 2026 at Olympia London, is one of the largest technology gatherings in Europe, drawing tens of thousands of attendees from more than ninety countries. It's exactly the kind of room where the stories I want to tell Taiwanese readers are being shaped — conversations about AI governance, security, infrastructure, and the international politics of technology that will eventually wash up on Taiwan's shores.
I'm requesting press access not to collect swag or selfies with keynote speakers, but to report. My plan is to file Mandarin-language coverage for [Blog Name] that does something most Taiwanese readers can't easily get: a firsthand account of what Europe's tech and policy community is actually saying, filtered through the question that matters most here — what does this mean for Taiwan?
I know I'm asking for a credential before I've built a large audience. Every publication faces that chicken-and-egg moment. What I can promise is straightforward: I will treat the access seriously, report honestly, credit my sources, and produce work I'd be glad to stand behind — the kind of careful, useful journalism this blog exists to provide.
That's the whole idea. A new outlet, a clear audience, an underserved language, and a genuine reason to be in the room.
If you're reading this as a fellow Taiwanese reader: thank you, and I hope this becomes a place you trust. And if you're reading this as part of London Tech Week's press team: I'd be grateful for the chance to do this work, and I think there's a real story here worth telling back home.
